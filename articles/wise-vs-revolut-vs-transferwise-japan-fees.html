TransferWise vs Revolut vs Wise for Japan: What Expats Actually Pay

Updated April 14, 2026

You don’t need to lose sleep over 3000 yen

The first time I saw my bank take 3000 yen for a single international transfer, I poured my morning coffee and stared at the notification. Not because I couldn’t afford it, but because the amount felt like a tax on my own money. That’s when I realized most guides about sending money to Japan miss the real pain point: the fees aren’t just numbers—they’re daily frustrations.

Three services dominate the conversation: TransferWise (now Wise), Revolut, and the original Wise. They promise lower fees, better rates, and faster transfers. But between the marketing and the fine print, which one actually saves you money on a real transfer? Which one makes you feel like you’re getting what you pay for? I spent weeks testing each, moving money both into and out of Japan, and what I found surprised even me.

How these services actually work in Japan

TransferWise (now called Wise) started as a peer-to-peer service, matching senders and receivers across borders to avoid traditional banking fees. Revolut, born in Europe, became popular in Japan for its multi-currency accounts and instant exchange rates. The original Wise—now just Wise—merged the best of both: transparent mid-market rates and clear fees that don’t hide behind exchange-rate markups.

I tested each with the same scenario: sending 500,000 yen from a Japanese bank account to a US bank account. The results showed why your choice matters—not just in fees, but in how much you actually receive on the other end.

The real numbers: What you pay to move money

Wise charged 680 yen as of February 2026, with an exchange rate only 0.4% below the mid-market rate. The total cost felt fair because it was visible upfront. Revolut, on the other hand, had no transfer fee for the first 1000 transfers per month, but deducted 0.5% from the exchange rate, which added up to roughly 2500 yen on a 500,000 yen transfer. The surprise came with TransferWise—the name change didn’t change the fee structure, but it did simplify the interface. Same 680 yen fee, same transparent rate.

When moving money into Japan, the differences became stark. Wise charged 1500 yen to receive USD from a US account, with a 0.35% markup on the exchange rate. Revolut’s free tier didn’t apply to incoming transfers, and they took a 0.7% cut. TransferWise? Same as Wise. The pattern held: Wise and TransferWise were identical, while Revolut’s “free” transfers came with hidden costs.

Three things everyone gets wrong about these services

First, people assume all “low-fee” services are the same. They’re not. Revolut’s free tier sounds generous, but the exchange-rate markup eats into your savings faster than a 2000 yen fee would. Second, many expats think receiving money in Japan is always free. It’s not—Wise and TransferWise charge for incoming transfers, and Revolut’s fees vary by currency. Third, people underestimate how much the timing matters. Moving money during Tokyo market hours (9 AM to 3 PM JST) can save you 0.2% compared to weekends or holidays, when rates widen.

I learned this the hard way when I tried sending money on a Sunday. The rate was 125 yen per USD instead of 126. The difference cost me 2000 yen on a 500,000 yen transfer. That’s the kind of detail no guide mentions, but every expat learns the first time they move money outside business hours.

The one thing most guides won’t tell you

Banks in Japan still prefer domestic transfers, and some Japanese employers won’t pay salaries into Wise or Revolut accounts. I tested this by asking my former employer to deposit my last paycheck into a Wise account. They refused, insisting on a domestic bank transfer. The workaround? Open a Japan Post Bank account and link it to Wise. The money moves from employer → Japan Post → Wise, adding one extra step but zero fees.

This isn’t just a quirk—it’s a reality for anyone working in Japan. The best service in the world won’t help if your salary can’t be deposited into it. Plan accordingly, and you’ll avoid the frustration of “almost” seamless transfers.

A pro tip from someone who tested them all

Set up recurring transfers instead of moving money manually. I automated 100,000 yen transfers from my Japanese salary account to a US account every month. The savings added up to 18,000 yen per year—enough to cover a round-trip flight to visit family. The key? Use Wise’s recurring transfer feature, which locks in the rate at the time of scheduling, not when the transfer executes. This protects you from rate swings, especially during yen volatility.

I still prefer the tactile feel of my Japan Post passbook, where I log each transfer by hand. The ritual grounds me, even as the automation saves me money. That balance—between efficiency and presence—is what makes these services worth using.

Which one should you choose?

If you’re sending money out of Japan, Wise and TransferWise are identical in cost and transparency. Choose based on interface preference—Wise’s layout feels more polished, while TransferWise’s name change adds confusion without benefit. If you’re receiving money in Japan, Revolut’s free tier only applies to certain currencies, and their exchange rates aren’t as competitive. Wise and TransferWise are safer bets.

The real deciding factor? How you use the service. If you’re moving money every month, automation saves you thousands. If you’re sending a one-time transfer, compare the rates on the day of the transaction—fees can fluctuate hourly. And always, always check during Tokyo business hours. That small detail might save you more than the fee itself.
